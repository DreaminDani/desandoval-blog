360 (VR) Teleconferencing with the Ricoh Theta S, WebGL and WebRTC

In this post I’ll describe how I took a half-baked idea to use a 360 camera as a webcam and turned it into a shippable feature within the Discuss.io platform. Over the course of a month, through a series of prototypes and user tests, I combined open-source technologies with the (paid) OpenTok service to create Discuss.io’s “360 Immersion” experience.

Out of the box

First, we bought the Ricoh Theta S — a consumer-grade 360 camera that we had tried at VR meetups.

It comes with a mobile app and streaming over USB. There were quite a few tutorials on how we might be able to get something useful out of it…

Here’s one: http://lists.theta360.guide/t/360-video-conferencing-with-the-ricoh-theta-s/38


I got the camera to work in spherical mode by connecting to a Mac or Windows laptop via USB and turning on “LIVE” mode (Power + Mode buttons):

Installing an additional .pkg (called UVC Blender) allows the two cameras to be automatically stitched together. Like so…

More details here (the Windows walkthrough is basically the same): http://theta360.guide/community-document/uvc-theta.html

The basic steps were:

  1. Install Live-Streaming app: https://theta360.com/en/support/download/
  2. Prompt to “register” appears. Plug in powered-off camera and click “Register”
  3. Press and hold “Power” and “Mode” at the same time until the “LIVE” icon appears on the face of the camera

Competitive Analysis

Since I was stuck, I took a look at how google handled the stretched view:

It seemed to adequately handle the wide aspect ratio of the UVC Blender-stitched imaged… so I inspected the page and found that they were adjusting the height/width of the video stream according to the input’s native resolution. So I copied what pixels they chose for my resolution (2047x628) and shoved it into our publishing code (commented out the code that forced subscribers to view others at a certain aspect ratio).

It worked perfectly:

Getting off the hot stuff

With publishing and subscribing in the wide aspect-ratio working, I was confident that I could get a Theta user talking with others in Discuss.io’s meeting room. My next problem would be to figure out how to do it without the UVC Blender software.

Why not just make every user install UVC blender?

  1. Because we’re a web app and everything should “just work” without having to install anything. The more overhead we have to getting a user connected to the platform, the harder it will be to support them
  2. Because it needs an amazing computer to process the video. Even on a 2014 Macbook Pro I was having a hard time getting above 10fps locally

So I jumped into the Theta community and found this three.js wrapper to support stitching theta streams in the browser:

https://github.com/mganeko/THETA_GL

The library worked out-of-the-box by pumping webRTC’s getUserMedia() into an HTML5 canvas element and applying UV Mapping to simulate full photospheres/equirectangular video.

I had to hack it a bit to get it to work with OpenTok and submitted a pull request to get that work back into the library: 

http://lists.theta360.guide/t/making-theta-360-work-with-opentoks-client-side-webrtc-livestreaming-mobile-stitching-google-cardboard-view/986

That PR has since been accepted and the Discuss.io code updated to support the “orbs” as an input. Updates that were made include:

  1. A new stream data object that tells all meeting room users that a participant is using a 360 camera
  2. A new UI element to let publishers/subscribers switch between “orb mode” (the raw camera output) and “360 mode”

Since the theta_gl library is based off of three.js, all of the VR functionality that comes with it can be included with a few lines of javascript. After a few user tests, I came up with icons to represent all of these modes and added them to our meeting room UI.

Attaching to a Tripod

To attach the Theta to a tripod, first screw the ¼” Expansion Screw Adapter into the device.

This will provide enough room between the bottom of the camera and the tripod mounting point to plug in the USB cable (as shown)

Once the USB cable and extension are securely connected, simply attach the device to the tripod of your choice.

Switching the Camera into “Live Stream” Mode

In order for Discuss.io to communicate with the RICOH Theta S, it must be turned on in the “Live Stream” mode. To do this, simply press the “Mode” button and the “Power” button at the same time for about 3 seconds:

Using 360 in the Meeting Room

To publish video from the 360-degree camera, you must select it from the “Audio/Video Settings” within the Discuss.io Meeting Room:

Clicking the “Audio/Video Settings” icon opens up the “Audio Video Settings” modal (shown above)

Select the “RICOH Theta S” from the “Webcam and Microphone” dropdowns (shown above)

The “Test Settings” button will display a preview of the entire 360 view from both cameras (shown below)

Click “Save” to update your Audio/Video Settings to use the RICOH Theta S input devices (shown above)

The webcam preview on the right side of the screen allows you to toggle between 360 modes (shown above)

You can click and drag around the 360 preview and click the same button to disable it (shown above)

When a 360-degree camera is shared, the same controls will appear for all other participants to click and drag around the 360 video stream (shown above)

All participants (including Moderators and Observers) will have their own 360 view to click and drag around — this can be disabled or enabled at any time (shown above)

Mobile and VR Views

Participants and observers on mobile (Android only) can view 360-degree participant streams in a full-screen “click and drag” environment:

Tapping the “360” button will enter a full screen mode (above)

To navigate around the view, simply tap and drag.

Rotating the device will show a landscape version of the 360 mode (shown below)

To exit 360 mode, drag from the side of the screen to show the android menu (shown below)


Tap the “back” button to exit fullscreen mode. In a later version, I added an “X” button to help those who weren’t used to the full screen mode.

VR View

VR mode is just a split view of the Android experience shown to the left. This lets users put their phone into a Google Cardboard compatible device for a VR-like experience.

This experience was demo-ed for brand researchers and managers at conferences around the world.

Closing Thoughts

All in all, I was happy with I was able to come up with in the few weeks I spent on this feature-set. Crunched for time (with conference season coming up), I had to make a lot of trade-offs to get the MVP out to the users.

The biggest challenges during work on this was developing a new interaction model (VR/Immersive video streams) within the current meeting room experience. The final UI had to support the new 360 features without disorienting new and experienced users.

I am excited for future iterations of the meeting room and this feature-set, especially with Safari’s recent announcement of WebRTC support.
